Golf is one of the sports in which people strive to improve. Many individuals would love to know how to emulate PGA tour players, what aspects they need to work on, and the most efficient way to become a great player.

Imagine, for a moment, standing on the green, visualizing each swing with the precision of a grandmaster, just like Collin Morikawa did at the illustrious ZOZO Championship in Chiba, Japan. This tournament, born in 2019, holds special significance for him as a Chinese-Japanese American, with his last name originating from Japan. It was a memorable event for him and his ancestors due to their connection to Japan.

In this blog, we will delve into Morikawa’s winning formula and explore what it takes to become a champion, according to the statistical data from the PGA Tour website.

Golf is a sport where victory isn’t rushed; triumph unfolds over days, not minutes. It is unlike the fast-paced courts of basketball or volleyball and demands endurance and laser focus. It’s a game that rewards those who master the art of patience and concentration.

Looking at the leaderboard for the tournament, you can already see the hint of why Morikawa became a champion.

As you can see, he had the best round, marked at 63, and he was the player with the lowest score for the final round among the top 10 players.

Also, take a closer look at the top 10 contenders. Most of the players who finished in the top 10 played better than they did in the third round on the last day. From the table, we can understand that you need to finish strong and play hard until the end if you want to win.

Now, let’s get into more detail. What’s the secret sauce behind those jaw-dropping scores? Of course, they need to make many birdies. However, in the PGA tour, all the players have the ability to get birdies. It’s a skill honed to precision. Then, what sets the champions apart from those who fall short, despite their birdie prowess?

Code 
library(tidyverse)

btb <- read_csv("~/OneDrive - University of Nebraska-Lincoln/Fall 2023/SPMC350 Fall2023/Blog/PGA STATS/Birdie to Bogey ratio.csv")

btb<-head(btb,10)

nbtb <- btb |> 
  select(PLAYER,`TOTAL BIRDIES AND BETTER`,`TOTAL BOGEYS AND WORSE`) |>
  pivot_longer(
    cols=-PLAYER, 
    names_to = "Score",
    values_to = "Total"
  )

ggplot() + 
  geom_bar(data=nbtb, aes(x=reorder(PLAYER, Total), weight=Total, fill=Score)) + 
  coord_flip() +
  labs(
    x="", 
    y="Count", 
    title="Offense or Defense: What's More Crucial?", 
    subtitle="Morikawa excels in birdies and minimizes bogeys, a top-tier player.", 
    caption="Source: PGATour Stats| By Miu Takahashi"
    ) +
  scale_fill_manual(values=c("red", "blue")) + 
  theme_minimal()+
  theme(
    plot.title = element_text(size = 20,face = "bold"),
       axis.title = element_text(size = 8), 
    plot.subtitle = element_text(size=10) )

From this data, Morikawa has the most number of birdies and also fewer bogeys compared to other players. It is important to make fewer mistakes and avoid the worst-case scenario in order to beat all players.

Let’s change the angle. Here’s an easy question: What do you need to make a birdie? It boils down to two essential moves—nailing that approach onto the green and then sinking the putt.

Code 
library(tidyverse)
library(ggrepel)

Putt <- read_csv("~/OneDrive - University of Nebraska-Lincoln/Fall 2023/SPMC350 Fall2023/Blog/PGA STATS/Putt.csv")

CMPutt <- Putt |>
  filter(PLAYER == "Collin Morikawa")

EC <- Putt |>
  filter(PLAYER == "Eric Cole")

BH <- Putt |>
  filter(PLAYER == "Beau Hossler")

RI <- Putt |>
  filter(PLAYER == "Ryo Ishikawa")

RS <- Putt |>
  filter(PLAYER == "Robby Shelton")

ggplot() + 
  geom_point(
    data=Putt, 
    aes(x=`AVG`, y=`GREENS HIT`), color = "grey") +
    geom_point(
    data=EC, 
    aes(x=`AVG`, y=`GREENS HIT`), color = "blue")+
   geom_point(
    data=RI, 
    aes(x=`AVG`, y=`GREENS HIT`), color = "blue")+
   geom_point(
    data=BH, 
    aes(x=`AVG`, y=`GREENS HIT`), color = "blue")+
   geom_point(
    data=RS, 
    aes(x=`AVG`, y=`GREENS HIT`), color = "blue")+
  geom_point(
    data=CMPutt, 
    aes(x=`AVG`, y=`GREENS HIT`), color ="red") +
  geom_text_repel(
    data=CMPutt,

 labs(
    x="Average Putt",

    title="Crack PGA's Winning Formula with Greens",

    caption="Source: PGATour Stats| By Miu Takahashi"
    ) +
  theme_minimal()+

    plot.title = element_text(size = 20,face = "bold"),
       axis.title = element_text(size = 8), 
    plot.subtitle = element_text(size=10) )

Morikawa’s greens hit number is 53, which ranks third, and his average putting is 1.604, ranking second. The blue dots represent the stats for the top 5 players, located in the left top corner, indicating that they also have the top level of green hits and average putt.

There are numerous aspects to winning and becoming a PGA Tour champion, but we can pinpoint the skills that are particularly important.
